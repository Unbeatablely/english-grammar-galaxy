# AI Doesn't Replace Us — It Redefines Who We Become

**英语阅读课演讲 · 经济新趋势**

---

We are living through the fastest economic transformation in human history. Artificial intelligence is no longer a distant possibility — it is reshaping industries, redefining professions, and rewriting the rules of value creation right now, in real time. From manufacturing floors to hospital diagnosis rooms, from financial trading desks to creative studios, AI has arrived. And with its arrival comes a wave of anxiety: *Will AI take my job? Will it make my skills obsolete? What is left for humans to do?*

These are urgent questions. But they are built on a flawed assumption — that the relationship between humans and AI is a zero-sum competition. History does not support this fear. Every major technological revolution in the past five hundred years has followed the same pattern: technology does not eliminate human work — it redefines it upward, toward creativity, judgment, and deeper insight.

Think about the printing press. When Gutenberg's invention spread across Europe in the 15th century, scribes panicked. Manuscript copying, a skilled trade practiced for centuries, was suddenly obsolete. But did the printing press destroy work? No — it created an entirely new ecosystem. Publishers, editors, typesetters, booksellers, authors, journalists. For every scribe displaced, more than ten new roles emerged. Knowledge became accessible to millions. The Renaissance accelerated. Human civilization advanced.

Think about the electronic spreadsheet. When VisiCalc and later Excel arrived in the 1980s, bookkeepers feared for their livelihoods. Why would a company pay someone to calculate rows of numbers by hand when software could do it instantly? But spreadsheets did not eliminate accounting — they elevated it. Bookkeepers became financial analysts. Freed from tedious calculation, they could interpret data, identify trends, and guide strategy. Nearly nine times more analytical roles emerged than the clerical jobs that were displaced.

Now consider AI. The World Economic Forum projects that by 2035, AI may displace approximately 85 million routine cognitive jobs — but it will also create an estimated 97 million new roles. The net effect is not destruction. It is transformation.

So what determines who thrives in this transformation? Here is the insight that matters most: in an AI-driven economy, the most scarce and valuable resource is not the ability to write code. It is the ability to ask the right questions. It is the capacity to judge what is good, what is true, and what is worth building. It is the power to connect ideas across different domains — to bridge technology and humanities, data and ethics, efficiency and beauty.

These are not engineering skills. These are human skills. And they are exactly what an education in the humanities cultivates.

I am an English major. Six months ago, I had never written a line of code. Today, I have built an interactive English grammar learning platform — an AI-powered tool that helps students understand complex grammar through visual, intuitive exploration. I did not become an engineer. I gave the AI direction. I described what I wanted. I made design judgments. I evaluated the output and refined it, over and over, until it was right. The AI executed. I directed. And the result is a live, functioning product that a year ago would have required a full team of developers.

This is the new reality. AI lowers the barrier between idea and execution to nearly zero. What used to require a CS degree now requires clarity of thought, good taste, and persistence. The scarce resource has shifted from technical implementation to human direction.

So do not ask: *Will AI replace me?* That question frames you as a passive object, as something that can be displaced. The right question — the one that defines the next generation of leaders, creators, and innovators — is this: *What do I want to redefine with AI?*

The new economy does not reward those who resist change. It rewards those who understand that AI is not a threat to human value — it is a multiplier of human intention. Your job is not to compete with the machine. Your job is to decide what the machine should build. And that decision — that act of judgment, taste, and vision — is irreducibly human.

The future belongs to those who ask better questions. Ask yours.

Thank you.

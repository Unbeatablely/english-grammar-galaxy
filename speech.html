AI Doesn't Replace Us —
It Redefines Who We
Can Become

A humanities student's journey into AI modeling,
and what it reveals about the future of work.

Scroll to read

Good morning, everyone.

Six months ago, if you had told me I would be building 3D models and training AI, I would have laughed. I study humanities. I read literature. I analyze texts. My world was words, not code.

But here I am — having just built something that combines both: an interactive English grammar learning tool, powered by AI-assisted modeling. And that journey taught me something crucial about the future of work.

We've all heard the fear: "AI is coming for our jobs." It's a headline that sells. But it's also a question built on the wrong assumption — the assumption that the relationship between humans and technology is a zero-sum game.

History tells a different story.

When the printing press arrived in the 15th century, scribes panicked. But printing didn't kill writing — it redefined it. Scribes became editors, publishers, and authors. The printing press eliminated about 30,000 scribe jobs across Europe — but it created over 300,000 new roles in publishing, bookselling, and journalism.

Ten times more jobs created than destroyed.

When spreadsheets were invented in the 1980s, bookkeepers feared obsolescence. Instead, they became financial analysts, freed from manual calculation to focus on strategy. About 400,000 bookkeeping jobs were displaced — but 3.5 million new roles emerged in data analysis, financial planning, and business intelligence.

Nearly nine times more.

Technology doesn't eliminate human work — it redefines it upward, toward creativity, judgment, and insight.

So what does AI redefine for us?

Here's the insight that changed everything for me: the most scarce resource in an AI-driven economy isn't coding ability. It's the capacity to ask the right questions, to judge what is "good," and to connect ideas across domains.

And these — asking questions, making judgments, connecting ideas — these are exactly what a humanities education trains us to do.

When I built that grammar tool, I didn't write thousands of lines of code. I described what I wanted. I made design decisions. I judged whether the output was pedagogically sound. The AI handled the implementation — I provided the direction.

The tool became proof that a literature student can create software — not because I became an engineer, but because engineering itself got redefined.

The question is no longer "Will AI replace me?" — that frames you as passive, as something that can be replaced.

The right question is: "What do I want to redefine with AI?"

Maybe you'll redefine how we learn history, by building immersive AI-powered timelines. Maybe you'll redefine how we access legal information, by creating AI agents that translate legalese into plain language. Maybe you'll redefine something none of us has even imagined yet.

Don't ask whether AI will replace you. Ask what you will redefine with it.

And if a humanities student can build an AI-powered grammar tool — imagine what you can build in your field.

Good morning, everyone.

Six months ago, if you told me a humanities student would be building AI models, I would have laughed. But here I am — I built an interactive English grammar tool, powered by AI. And that journey changed how I see the future of work.

We all know the fear: "AI is coming for our jobs." But history tells a different story.

◈ Show Chart
Slide — Data Visualization

When the printing press arrived, scribes panicked — yet it created ten times more jobs than it destroyed. When spreadsheets appeared, bookkeepers feared obsolescence — but 3.5 million new roles emerged in analysis and strategy.

Every tech revolution follows the same pattern: technology doesn't eliminate work. It redefines it upward — toward creativity, judgment, and insight.

Here's what I learned: the most scarce resource in an AI economy isn't coding. It's the ability to ask the right questions, judge what is good, and connect ideas across fields.

Sound familiar? That's exactly what a humanities education trains us to do.

When I built that grammar tool, I didn't write code. I described what I wanted. I made design choices. The AI executed — I directed.

A literature student built software — not by becoming an engineer, but because engineering itself got redefined.

So don't ask: "Will AI replace me?"

Ask: "What do I want to redefine with AI?"

If I can build this — imagine what you can build in your field.
